<h2 id="research">Research</h2>
<p>Computer programming is a huge success; it plays a major role in most modern problem solving. However, for some problems we still have trouble writing programs that reliably do the right thing. My research improves software reliability by developing tools that help programmers ensure their code is safe and accurate.</p>
<p>My students and I focus on important programs, including the software infrastructure that many other programs rely on (compilers, distributed systems, networks); control programs in safety-critical applications (radiotherapy devices); and approximations used in engineering and manufacturing (floating point, 3D printing). We spend many hours working out proofs on the whiteboard, but we always build working systems. We <a href="https://github.com/uwplse">release our code</a> so others can review and build on our work, and we get free food when we work late.</p>
<p>All our <a href="#publications">papers</a> and <a href="#projects">projects</a> are collaborations with incredible <a href="#collaborators">colleagues</a>. Our home base is in the vibrant <a href="http://uwplse.org/">UW PLSE</a> group and we also have long term collaborations with <a href="https://sampl.cs.washington.edu/">SAMPL</a>, <a href="https://syslab.cs.washington.edu/">Syslab</a>, the <a href="https://adacenter.org/index.php/">Applications Driving Architectures (ADA)</a> Center, and the <a href="http://sandcat.cs.washington.edu/">SandCat Project</a>.</p>

<h2 id="background">Background</h2>
<p>My research interests include formal verification, compilers, and security. Even outside the lab, I try to code for fun most days. If I’m not hacking, I’m running with friends or making something. I can juggle and solve Rubik’s cubes, but not at the same time. Also, my name has a <a href="http://totalcrazyhack.net/">wicked cool anagram</a>.</p>
<p>I spent six sunny years at <a href="http://cse.ucsd.edu/">UC San Diego</a> working on the PhD with my incredible advisor <a href="http://cseweb.ucsd.edu/~lerner/">Sorin Lerner</a>. Throughout grad school, Sorin set a stellar example of how remarkable research can be when you put students first, an example I strive to emulate. I also learned many invaluable lessons from the great <a href="https://ranjitjhala.github.io/">Ranjit Jhala</a>, especially when it comes to writing and presentation: <em>Less is more!</em></p>
<p>I graduated from <a href="http://www.purdue.edu/">Purdue University</a> back in Spring 2007 with degrees in <a href="http://www.cs.purdue.edu/">Computer Science</a> and <a href="http://www.math.purdue.edu/">Mathematics</a>. As an undergraduate, I was fortunate to perform research with <a href="https://www.cs.purdue.edu/homes/suresh/">Suresh Jagannathan</a> on the SML compiler <a href="http://mlton.org/">MLton</a>. For our Honors Project, advised by <a href="http://hosking.github.io/">Antony Hosking</a>, <a href="https://www.cc.gatech.edu/~wharris/">Bill Harris</a> and I designed and implemented a domain specific language to control a giant neon sculpture over the web. Over nearly three years, I ran the lab component of Purdue’s introductory Java programming course.</p>
