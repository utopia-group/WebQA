<h3><a name="projects">Current Research Projects</a></h3>
<i>Verified Software</i>
<div style="padding-left:25px;padding-right:20%;">
Despite lots of testing, bugs are rampant in
modern software systems.  How do we make it cost-effective
to <i>verify</i> critical correctness properties of programs?  This
question motivates a lot of my research; current directions
include <a href="http://web.cs.ucla.edu/~todd/research/pldi16.pdf">automatic
    inference of program properties</a>
and <a href="http://web.cs.ucla.edu/~todd/research/icml18.pdf">abstractions
    for reasoning about probabilistic programs</a>.</div><br/>
<i>Simple and Efficient Concurrency</i>
<div style="padding-left:25px;padding-right:20%;">
Mainstream programming languages like C++ and Java provide an
incredibly complex and error-prone semantics for threads in the name
of efficiency.  Can we instead provide the simple interleaving
semantics of threads (known formally as <i>sequential consistency</i>)
at a reasonable cost?  <a href="http://www.cs.ucla.edu/~todd/research/snapl15.pdf">Start here</a> for
the high-level problem and motivation.  In recent work we built a <a href="http://web.cs.ucla.edu/~todd/research/pldi19.pdf">sequentially
consistent Java virtual machine</a> and measured its performance.</div><br/>
<i>Network Programming and Verification</i>
<div style="padding-left:25px;padding-right:20%;">
Computer networks have become critical infrastructure for all Internet-based services, and as a result the requirements on and complexity of networks have rapidly increased.  Can programming languages technology help to manage this complexity?  We are working on both <a href="http://www.cs.ucla.edu/~todd/research/nsdi15_batfish.pdf">verification</a> of existing networks and <a href="http://www.cs.ucla.edu/~todd/research/sigcomm16.pdf">new languages</a> to design networks that are correct by construction.
</div><br/>

</p><h3><a name="bio">Brief Biography</a></h3>

I joined the UCLA faculty in November 2003.
I am also a co-founder and Chief Scientist
of <a href="https://www.intentionet.com">Intentionet</a>.
I have been an Academic Visitor at Oxford (2010-11), a Visiting
Researcher at Microsoft Research (2012), and a Visiting
Fellow at Princeton (2018-19).
I received my PhD from the <a href="http://www.washington.edu/">University of Washington</a>
Department of <a href="http://www.cs.washington.edu">Computer
Science</a>, where  I was a member of the Cecil
group led by <a href="https://research.google.com/pubs/author4707.html">Craig
Chambers</a>.
Before that, I was an undergraduate at <a href="http://www.brown.edu">Brown University</a>, where I was advised
by <a href="http://www.cs.brown.edu/~pck">Paris Kanellakis</a> and
<a href="http://pwp.gatech.edu/pascal-van-hentenryck/">Pascal Van Hentenryck</a>.  I
grew up in suburban Maryland, outside of Washington D.C.<br/><br/>

I received an NSF CAREER award in 2006, an IBM Faculty Award in 2008, the
<a href="http://www.sigplan.org/Awards/PLDI">Most Influential PLDI
  Paper Award</a> in 2011, an IEEE Micro Top Picks selection in 2012,
the Northrop
    Grumman Excellence in Teaching Award from UCLA Engineering in
  2016, 
a <a href="https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/academic-program/outstanding-collaborator-award">Microsoft
    Research Outstanding Collaborator Award</a> in 2016, and
  an <a href="http://okawa-foundation.or.jp/en/index.html">Okawa
  Foundation</a> Research Grant in 2016.
