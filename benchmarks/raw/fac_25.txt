<h3><a name="bio">Biography</a></h3>
<p>I'm an Associate Professor in the Computer Science and Electrical 
Engineering Departments of Stanford University.   
I head the <a href="http://sing.stanford.edu">Stanford Information Networking
      Group (SING)</a> 
and co-direct <a href="http://maker.stanford.edu/lab64">lab64</a>, the EE maker space at Stanford.
      I co-directed the <a href="http://iot.stanford.edu">Secure Internet of Things Project</a>.  
I research operating systems, networks, and software design, especially
for embedded systems.
I appreciate excellent engineering
and have a self-destructive aversion to low hanging fruit. 
</p>
<p>
The results of my research are used by thousands of people, run 
on hundreds of thousands 
of devices, and are the basis for <a href="http://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6550">Internet</a> <a href="http://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6206">standards</a>.
I written <a href="http://csl.stanford.edu/~pal/pubs.html">some papers</a>.
I've been awarded an NSF CAREER Award and a 
<a href="http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/collaboration/awards/msrff_all.aspx#2008">Microsoft New Faculty Fellowship</a>.  In 2010, three of my advisees,
<a href="http://web.cse.ohio-state.edu/~kannan/">Kannan</a>,
<a href="https://sing.stanford.edu/site/users/16">Mayank</a>, 
and <a href="https://sing.stanford.edu/site/users/17">Jung Il</a>, 
had the bold idea that <a href="http://news.stanford.edu/news/2011/february/duplex-radio-transmission-021411.html">it's possible to build</a>
a <a href="https://sing.stanford.edu/site/publications/48">full duplex radio</a> and 
founded <a href="http://www.kumunetworks.com">Kumu Networks</a> to
commercialize it. 
I place all of my work funded industrially in the public domain.
</p>

<h3>TinyOS Programming Manual</h3>
I wrote a short book on programming TinyOS. 
<a href="pubs/tinyos-programming.pdf">Programming TinyOS</a> digs into nesC and how you can use it to build TinyOS applications. 
A second version of the text,
co-written with David Gay, is <a href="http://www.cambridge.org/us/catalogue/catalogue.asp?isbn=9780521896061">available for purchase</a> as of April 2009. We tried to keep the cost
down by making it softcover and having very tiny royalties. Unfortunately,
though, TinyOS programming is not a blockbuster topic, so the book is
a bit pricier than I'd like. You can download the <a href="pubs/tos-programming-web.pdf">
first half of the published version</a> for free. The first half covers the basics: this
version does not include advanced topics like asynchronous code, writing generics, or
the hardware abstraction architecture. There's a short <a href="tinyos-errata.txt">errata page</a>.
