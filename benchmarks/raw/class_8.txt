<p class="title1"><span style='mso-bidi-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Course
Overview<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal">CS 111, Operating System Principles is meant to provide you with
a solid grounding in the basic principles of operating system design.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>This grounding will give you a deeper
understanding of how a vital piece of system software operates and introduce
you to important concepts that are frequently used in large software systems
you are likely to encounter in your future work.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><o:p> </o:p></p>
<p class="MsoNormal">CS 111 has prerequisites of CS 32, 33, and 35L.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>The class involves serious programming and
would be extremely challenging for anyone without a programming
background.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>It also assumes knowledge of
many fundamental tools used in software development, particularly in Linux/Unix
environments.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>If you have not taken
these courses or otherwise acquired this background, you are likely to find CS
111 very difficult.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><o:p> </o:p></p>
<p class="MsoNormal">CS 111 is widely held to be one of the most difficult
courses in the undergraduate Computer Science catalog, due to:</p>

</span></span></span>the amount of reading</p>

</span></span></span>the number of new and subtle concepts to be

<p class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="margin-left:1.5in;mso-add-space:
auto;text-indent:-.25in;mso-list:l0 level1 lfo2"><span style="font-family:Symbol;mso-fareast-font-family:Symbol;mso-bidi-font-family:

<p class="title1"><span style='mso-bidi-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Academic
honesty<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="subtitle1"><span style='mso-bidi-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>The
policy—avoid cheating!<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<div style="margin-top:3.75pt;margin-bottom:3.75pt">
<p class="MsoNormal">I expect all students to follow the <a href="http://www.deanofstudents.ucla.edu/Portals/16/Documents/144317_condeofcoduct_MarchV2_final%20with%20links.pdf">UCLA
Student Conduct Code</a>. This code prohibits cheating, fabrication, multiple
submissions, and facilitating academic dishonesty. You can find further
information about this code at the <a href="http://www.deanofstudents.ucla.edu/Portals/16/Documents/StudentGuide.pdf">Student
Guide to Academic Integrity</a>.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>The <a href="http://www.deanofstudents.ucla.edu/">Office of the Dean of Students</a>
offers a <a href="http://www.deanofstudents.ucla.edu/Video">workshop on
academic integrity</a> if you wish to understand UCLA’s policies on this issue
more thoroughly.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">Group study is often useful and is encouraged, but projects
are to be performed by each student individually, except for project 3, which
can be performed with one partner.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>You
are not permitted to use any other students’ code or written material in your
projects (other than your partner’s for project 3), and you should not give
your code or written material to any other students.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>You are not permitted to post your code to <span class="SpellE">Github</span> or to other web sites.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>In cases where multiple projects show signs
of plagiarism, all involved parties will be reported to the Dean, so even if it
was your work that was copied, you could still face consequences.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Brief quotations from other sources are
sometimes acceptable in project submissions, but submissions should be
predominantly your own original work.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>If
you include any material in your project submissions that is obtained from an
online source, book, or other place, you must specify the sources for these
parts of your submitted work.</p>
